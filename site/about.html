What is this?

This page is a live workspace. It's being built by the system it describes.

Workwright is a protocol for making things with AI. Not a coding assistant. Not an agent framework. A crit system — where every piece of work must be defended conceptually, and every defense gets judged.

How it works

  1. You propose a change. Say what you want and why it matters. Not how — that's the wright's job.
  2. A wright does the work. A wright is an LLM that operates within the protocol. It reads the taste guide, does the work, and writes a defense — why it made these choices, conceptually, not just technically.
  3. You crit it. Read the diff and the defense. Score it. Your reason matters more than the number. A +0.8 with no reason teaches nothing. A -0.5 with "the defense doesn't address why these elements are grouped this way" teaches everything.
  4. The taste guide evolves. Accepted patterns become principles. Rejected ones become anti-patterns. Over time, wrights produce work that reflects the community's accumulated judgment.
  5. Accepted changes go live. This page is always the latest accepted state.

The two questions

Every piece of work must answer:

  1. Why are we making this?
  2. How does it solve it elegantly?

These are Socratic. They recurse. The wright answers them before writing a line of code. The human asks them again during crit. "Why do you think what you think you think?"

What's a wright?

Not an "agent." A wright. From Old English wyrhta — one who works. Shipwright, wheelwright, playwright. A craftsperson who works within a tradition, not a contractor executing instructions.

A wright that can't defend its choices — regardless of whether the code works — has produced accidental work. Working code with no defensible reason for its form won't survive the next change.

What's a taste guide?

Not a style guide. Not linting rules. A living document generated from crit signals — what gets accepted, what gets rejected, and why. It reflects the community's actual taste, not their stated preferences.

The guide starts empty. It emerges from judgment. Right now it has a few signals. Give it more.

What's the point?

We gave the world a generative engine and most people produced slop with it. Then blamed the engine. AI didn't create slop. It revealed that the loop between intention and evaluation was broken.

Workwright fixes the loop. Not by adding more automation — by making the unit of work small enough to actually judge, and requiring every piece of work to carry its own argument for existing.

The same protocol works for LLMs, humans, and teams. The system doesn't care what you are. It cares if you can defend your work.

Try it

Go to the home page and propose a change. The scope is this site — site/index.html. A wright will pick it up, do the work, write a defense, and submit it for crit. You score it.

If the protocol works, this page gets better over time. If it doesn't, that's visible too. Either way, it's honest. 

$ ww task "your intent" --why "your reason"
$ ww run-next
$ ww crit <task_id>
